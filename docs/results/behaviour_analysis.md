# Behavioural & Written Feedback Analysis

> Data source: `interaction_events` table (3,672 events) + `support_feedback.comments_other`.
> Generated by `pull_behaviour_data.py`. 90 sessions with telemetry (54 Group A, 36 Group B).

---

## 1. Behavioural Metrics

| Metric | Group A | Group B | Δ (A − B) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sessions with telemetry | 54 | 36 | — |
| **Avg session duration** | **323s (5.4 min)** | **428s (7.1 min)** | **B +105s** |
| **Avg user turns** | **3.9** | **4.4** | B +0.5 |
| **Avg typing duration / turn** | **19,204ms (19s)** | **12,134ms (12s)** | **A +7s** |
| Avg reply latency | 3,922ms | 3,517ms | B −405ms |

### Interpretation

**Group B sessions ran longer with more back-and-forth.** Users in Group B averaged 4.4 user turns vs 3.9 in Group A, and sessions lasted 105 seconds longer on average. This suggests the confirmation-style responses required more follow-up to get to a satisfying answer — users had to re-prompt more often.

**Group A users wrote significantly more per turn.** Average typing duration of 19 seconds vs 12 seconds in Group B. This likely reflects that Group A's more conversational, empathetic responses encouraged richer, more expressive replies from users. It could also reflect users engaging more naturally rather than issuing short commands.

**Reply latency is similar.** A 3.9s vs 3.5s average is within expected variance and reflects server processing rather than variant differences. Both groups experienced responsive service.

## 2. Written Feedback Analysis

Free-text comments from `support_feedback.comments_other`. Excludes comments about UI/rendering bugs (numbered lists all showing "1", responses cut off, HTML artefacts) as these are display issues unrelated to the response quality.

### Group A — 31 responses

**Over-empathy / tone fatigue** (4 responses)

Users noticed and disliked the explicit empathy, despite rating it well on the star scale:

- *"It was also kind of annoying how overly 'understanding' it was."*
- *"Tone feels too forced. Every response starts with the system saying it understands the situation, personally feels forced and repetitive after a while."*
- *"I don't need it to emphasise with my problem and tell me it's frustrating — I'd rather it just offer straight support."*
- *"The second response may be a little \*too\* empathetic, but that's more personal preference of course."*

**Positive: resolution and accuracy** (7 responses)

- *"It gave me accurate information and resolved the 'issue'."*
- *"Very clear plans that could suit anyone and outlined what was in those mobile plans."*
- *"Amazing info re possible phones both new and second hand!"*
- *"Good response when customer stated first attempts didn't work. Really good scenario and positive outcome."*
- *"Clear, reliable information provided."*
- *"Overall, very effective system."*

**Billing / account access limitation** (4 responses, both groups)

Users in both groups who had billing scenarios flagged that the assistant couldn't resolve specific charges. This is scenario-driven, not variant-driven:

- *"It's hard to resolve this without knowing why the bill has had an extra charge."*
- *"I started just confused with the bill and it offered many reasonable solutions... then I asked how can I be sure and it just looped back to me looking at the bill."*
- *"The agent did not really help with my issue and was not able to be specific with why I was overcharged."*

**Accuracy concerns** (3 responses)

- *"At the start it just kind of said random names of generic data plans... which kinda felt made up."*
- *"It doesn't know the details on what the possible plans are... if it knows the details on plans it should surely know further details."*
- *"I don't know how reliable the prices are."*

### Group B — 18 responses

**Generic / vague / untailored responses** (5 responses)

The most consistent Group B complaint — responses answered questions but lacked specificity:

- *"Was hoping for specific info relating to VodaCare's services, rather than general advice."*
- *"Expected more specific information about VodaCare. Asked it about my actual provider, gave me the wrong number."*
- *"It just gave me the same answers again and again — even when I tried to ask about Airalo — wasn't tailored."*
- *"I would say the advice/suggestions that the bot gave was a bit vague as a non-technical person."*
- *"Spoke about plans but originally did not explain them."*
- *"The chat box gave broad help, but told me to get help from a manager or another chat box."*

**Context and memory issues** (2 responses)

- *"Agent forgot context after leaving app for a bit."*
- *"I found this a bit more confusing."* (after a multi-turn billing session)

**Some positive / partial** (3 responses)

- *"A wide range of options provided though, with some good things to try."*
- *"Information on the plans that had been completed was very clear."*
- *"Told me what I needed."*

## 3. Cross-Cutting Findings

### 3a. Session efficiency vs depth

Group B required more turns and more time to reach the same outcome on task success (LAJ: 0.83 both groups). The confirmation prompt produces responses that users need to follow up on more often — either because answers felt incomplete, generic, or required clarification. Group A's kindness prompt appears to front-load more complete answers in fewer turns.

### 3b. The over-empathy signal in Group A

Group A's written feedback surfaces a tension not visible in the star ratings: some users explicitly found the constant empathy expressions annoying or robotic, despite rating empathy highly on the 1–5 scale. Four users in Group A specifically flagged this. This suggests that while the Kindness prompt reliably produces empathetic language that both users and the LAJ rate positively, a subset of users find the style repetitive or patronising in text form.

This is a nuance not captured by aggregate star ratings: average empathy self-rating of 4.50 in Group A hides a minority who found it excessive. Group B's lower empathy rating (3.90) reflects a clearer and more consistent perception — users simply found it lacking rather than being split.

### 3c. Specificity gap in Group B

Group B's primary written-feedback complaint is vagueness — generic advice, repeated answers, failure to tailor to the user's specific provider or situation. This did not surface clearly in the star ratings (task success 4.06 both groups) but is a consistent theme in free text. Group A received fewer complaints about vagueness, suggesting the Kindness prompt's more conversational style also yields more contextualised responses.

### 3d. Shared limitations (scenario-driven)

Both groups share: billing scenarios where account access is impossible, and occasional response truncation. These are system limitations that affect both variants equally and explain the floor on task success scores in both groups.
